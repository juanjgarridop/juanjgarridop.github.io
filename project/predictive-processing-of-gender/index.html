Predictive Processing of Gender Agreement

May 1, 2019

There is evidence that native speakers of Spanish use gender-marked articles to predict upcoming nouns during online processing (Lew-Williams & Fernald, 2007, 2010). However, other studies on adult second language (L2) learners found that L2 learners do not use grammatical gender to predict upcoming information, or at least, not in the same way as native speakers do. This difference seems to depend on different factors such as target gender assignment (Hopp, 2012), the strength of lexical representations (Hopp, 2016), the number of cues available (Fowler & Jackson, 2017), among others.

In the present study, I explore whether adult L2 learners and native speakers of Spanish use grammatical gender predictively during online processing. Particularly, I examine whether and under what conditions native and non-native speakers of Spanish use gender-marked articles and nouns to identify upcoming nouns and adjectives. This study explores the following research questions: 1) Do English-speaking adult L2 learners of Spanish process gender agreement in Spanish predictively? If so, do target-like gender assignment and agreement in production and comprehension correlate with the use of grammatical gender for predictive processing? 2) Does the number of gender cues available affect the use of grammatical gender for predictive processing? 3) Does the level of proficiency have an effect on the predictive use of gender?

Thirty adult English-speaking learners and thirty native speakers of Spanish will perform a picture-naming task and a picture-identification task to test gender assignment and agreement in production and comprehension, and an eye-tracking task to test predictive processing of grammatical gender. I predict that (1) only the participants who show mastery of gender assignment and agreement will be able to use gender predictively, (2) participants will be more likely to use gender cues predictively when they have a higher number of cues available, and (3) the higher the proficiency level, the more participants will exploit gender cues for predictions.

This study contributes to our understanding of how L2 learners process gender information during online comprehension and what factors might intervene in using grammatical gender during L2 predictive processing.
